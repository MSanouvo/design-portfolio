Schoolhouse Ghana

Role

Team Researcher

Task

I was tasked with conducting a majority of the research for the team throughout the project. I provided research on the environment and weather conditions surrounding the school as well as various solutions that could be applied to similar scenarios. As well as providing research, I assisted in brainstorming ideas as well as creating designs for the prototype. As we grew closer towards our end design, I looked for inexpensive and sustainable materials that could be used for the final product.​​​​​​​

Background

While working with UCSD's Global TIES foundation in one of my classes, I practiced designing in a team-based environment throughout a 10 week period. For my final project, I worked with the GodFred's foundation and helped prevent a school in Ghana from having rain leak into their class spaces. In the course of about 8 weeks, I was assigned to a small team of 4 and worked through brainstorming, iterating, and prototyping a product with sustainability in mind.

Project Detail

The GodFreds Foundation is a non-profit organization that aims to build low-budget private schools for underprivileged kids to provide positive learning environments in Ghana. Working with the GodFreds Foundation, my team was assigned to design a solution to stop rainfall that continually entered the classrooms of the school building during stormy seasons. During these seasons, water leaked through the wood panel windows and flood open spaces used by the children in other parts of the school. Designing a solution would help maintain the learning environment of the children who already face many hardships outside of school.

Since the GodFreds Foundation was a non-profit organization, we had to be conscious of the cost that our solution would present. As we were planning out our potential design solutions, we wanted to make sure they could be built out of cost-efficient, sustainable resources.

Concepts/Brainstorming

The first step in my brainstorming process was looking at existing options to the the problem at hand. Researching ways to protect groups of people from rainfall, my team and I created pros and cons list for preexisting solutions - this varied from simple options such as tents to larger solutions like hurricane shutters.

Using the knowledge from the list of existing solutions, I started to brainstorm possible concepts that could be applied to the problem. I was interested in the idea of creating a mini storm shutter attachment to place over the window, but upon further discussion with the team, we thought that it would've been difficult to find suitable materials and install. Other notable concepts while collaborating with my team included a deployable tarp with different applications.

After comparing all the notable ideas using a design matrix, we as a whole decided that a drop-down deployable tarp was the best concept to implement in response to our problem scenario.

Analysis and Testing

After having decided on our concept going forward, my team began working on analyzing the design and testing its effectiveness. In order to do this, things we considered were how well does our design work at blocking out rain and is it a sustainable design that would survive a long time in the climate surrounding the area.

My task was analyzing the cost of the materials we could use. I researched various materials that our client could be used to build the final product. While researching, I focused on the cost of the materials as well as their quality, making sure we would be able to send a detailed list of cheap, yet sustainable materials so our design would last the school for many years.

While I was doing research on materials, the rest of my group built a miniature prototype to test out the feasibility of our design. Due to our lack of resources and engineering skills, they made the prototype with only cardboard and plastic wrap, using the materials to act as the school and the tarp. Spraying water over the prototype to simulate rainfall, we concluded that the design was feasible enough to move forward.

Design and Implementation

With the constraints we faced during the course of the project, the team decided to focus on one area of the school that seemed to be affected by the rain the most based on information shared by the client. Between the impact from the rain leaking into the windows and falling into the open courtyard, we agreed that addressing the courtyard situation was more important.

Following our feasibility test using the cardboard mock up and the research done during the previous stage of our project, we proceeded to compile a list of materials for our client to use as they implemented the final product on their end. Our deliverables included an outline specifying the design as well as a list of materials to order including their cost and manufacturer. The issue we addressed in our solution was the courtyard which saw rain flooding in it during stormy seasons. In order to prevent the courtyard from flooding, we designed a cheap, yet sustainable awning tarp that could easily be installed over the opening of the courtyard to shield the inside from the rainfall.

Our implementation process involved passing on our current work and findings onto our partner organization to gain their approval. From there, they would oversee the rest of the project while we would offer support to their implementation. Being a non-profit organization, we helped our partners figure out way to receive donations and funding as they did when originally building the school while keeping their donors engaged throughout the implementation process with updates and social media coverage. Overall we worked on their desires to the best of our ability to offer them the best product we could design for their situation.

Conclusion and Reflection

Due to lack of time, resources, and manpower, we were tasked to hand off the final design details to our client as the class came to an end so they could continue to implement our final product onto their school. There was still a lot that could've been done for our product and its design, but our time in the program was coming to an end and we had to wrap up the existing work we had already made progress on.

Looking back at the project, there were definitely many hurdles we had to jump just to get started. Some of these obstacles included lack of communication with our partner in the initial stages of the project alongside insufficient information due to the preceding problem. Our team went ahead and made educated decisions on how to proceed. We looked up relevant information and made our decisions based off of what we thought would be best for the school - trying to empathize their needs. These problems left me to believe that the first few weeks of the project were put to waste as I personally was unsure of the direction of our design and whether we were making the right product that our stakeholders had in mind.
